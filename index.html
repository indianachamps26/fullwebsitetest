The Monthly High-Five

Five March headlines. Zero fluff. The moves that matter.

1
M&A
QXO Closes $2.25B Kodiak Deal — Eyes $50B Empire by 2031
QXO closed its acquisition of Kodiak Building Partners in late March, combining Beacon's roofing/exterior platform with Kodiak's $2.4B lumber, trusses, gypsum and structural products base. Valued at ~10.7x projected 2025 EBITDA (7.3x with synergies). QXO now eyes Boise Cascade, BlueLinx and US LBM as next targets with ~$10B in remaining war chest.
⚡ QXO's addressable market just expanded to $200B+. The consolidation playbook is no longer just about roofing.

Materials
Major Manufacturers Fire Coordinated Price Increases — Effective Late March/April
Atlas Roofing (5–8%), CertainTeed (up to 8%), and TAMKO announced price increases on shingles, underlayment and ventilation — effective March 23–April 15, 2026, regardless of prior order entry date. Aluminum mill shapes up 33% YoY per AGC; steel mill products rose 20.7% YoY as of January 2026.
⚡ Contractors with open bids from pre-March proposals are absorbing margin gaps in real time. Lock in material pricing before Q2 deliveries.

Regulatory
DOL Moves to Rescind 2024 Independent Contractor Rule
In late February — confirmed via March legal roundup — the DOL's Wage and Hour Division proposed rescinding the 2024 independent contractor rule, returning toward the narrower 2021 standard. This shift favors a broader definition of independent contractor status, potentially reducing W-2 reclassification risk for subcontractor-heavy platforms.
⚡ Welcome relief for PE platforms with 1099-heavy labor models — but "pending" means nothing is locked. Continue modeling both scenarios.

Midwest Roll-Up Activity Accelerates: Royalty Roofing, Ridgeline & More
March saw continued bolt-on activity in the Midwest: Royalty Roofing USA expanded its commercial footprint by acquiring Division 7 across Michigan; Ridgeline Roofing & Restoration acquired Bold North Roofing in the Twin Cities/western Wisconsin market. Both moves reflect geographic density strategies prioritized by PE buyers in high-storm-frequency corridors.
⚡ Midwest density plays are accelerating ahead of spring storm season. Target-rich geography meets non-discretionary demand cycle.

ABC/BLS PPI data through January 2026 showed nonresidential construction input prices up 2.9% YoY — annualized January rate described by ABC as a "blistering" 7.1%. ⚡ Operators without fixed-price supplier contracts are entering spring with exposed margins. Procurement strategy is now a competitive advantage.
Retrospective

Lessons from the Ladder

History this industry either learned from — or repeats.

The First Roll-Up Wave: What They Got Wrong (Late 1990s–Early 2000s)

The first institutional wave imposed uniform labor models on hyper-local markets and centralized procurement before systems existed to manage it. They paid premium multiples for revenue they couldn't retain post-transition. Result: value destruction, operator exodus after earnout, and a generation of PE skeptics in the trades.

Platforms that survived share one trait: they bought the operator as much as the business — retaining founders with real equity and building tech infrastructure before they needed it.

🎓 Earnout structures that ignore workforce continuity are the sector's #1 value destroyer.

Katrina (2005) & the 2011 Super Outbreak

Katrina: Gulf Coast labor spiked 40–60% in 90 days. Lead times stretched 6+ months. Undercapitalized shops couldn't manage materials, crews, and insurance timelines simultaneously — a demand surge that ruined the unprepared.

2011: 358 tornadoes, $11B insured losses. PE investments in AL/TN/MS in 2012–13 were acquired at peak storm-cycle multiples. When demand normalized, the underlying business was smaller than the thesis assumed.

🎓 Storm EBITDA ≠ recurring EBITDA. Always normalize the trailing twelve.

Tech Spotlight — March 2026

Things To Watch: EagleView One

Each month we spotlight one tool, platform, or technology worth understanding — whether you're an operator, a PE investor evaluating platforms, or both.

If you've been in roofing for more than a few years, you know EagleView as the company that produces the aerial measurement reports your estimators order before every job. That description no longer does it justice. In March 2026, EagleView formally launched EagleView One — a unified, subscription-based platform combining high-precision aerial imagery, AI-powered analytics, and full 3D property modeling into a single interface. The company is positioning it not as a reporting tool but as a property intelligence platform — and the distinction matters.

The headline capability is 3D exterior measurement at 98.77% claimed accuracy, now covering roofs, walls, windows, and doors for both residential and commercial properties. For PE platforms managing multi-crew operations across geographies, it means standardized measurement inputs that survive crew and estimator turnover. EagleView's CEO Piers Dormeyer put it plainly: the goal is to be the single source of truth for every property-related workflow, from estimate to insurance claim to predictive maintenance scheduling.

The March 12th live event introduced virtual collaboration features — multiple stakeholders accessing and manipulating the same 3D property model simultaneously. EagleView also announced EagleView Labs, a dedicated AI innovation hub, and appointed Dr. Dylan Kesler to lead AI research — signaling that the product roadmap is investment-backed, not marketing theater.

What it measures: Total daily revenue ÷ active crews on site. A crew = 3–5 workers plus a lead. Reveals crew sizing, job sequencing, and material delivery efficiency in one number.

Why PE buyers care: $8K+ across 5+ crews means labor productivity, scheduling, and logistics are solved. Below $5K reveals hidden scope creep, rework, and downtime invisible in the P&L until after close.

March relevance: With Atlas, CertainTeed, and TAMKO raising prices 5–8% in late March, operators tracking RPCD daily catch margin compression at crew level before it hits quarterly EBITDA.

Watch-out: High residential RPCD with low commercial exposure misleads — residential runs faster but is storm-dependent. Normalize for mix before cross-market comparison.

Beyond the Shingle Bottom Line — March 2026

March delivered a clear message: the consolidation window is compressing from both ends simultaneously. Above the market, QXO closed Kodiak and now controls a $200B+ addressable market with ~$10B still to deploy — and it has named its next targets. Inside the market, Atlas, CertainTeed, and TAMKO executed coordinated 5–8% price increases effective the final week of March, hitting every contractor with open bids and no locked-in material pricing.

The DOL's move to rescind the 2024 independent contractor rule offers PE platforms a potential reprieve on W-2 reclassification exposure. It is permission to get your documentation in order while the regulatory window is favorable. The rule is pending, not resolved.

The operators entering Q2 in the strongest position spent March doing three things: locking material pricing before April effective dates, tracking RPCD to catch margin compression at the crew level, and closing the gap between their estimating cycle and the market's repricing cycle. Know your RPCD before a buyer's diligence team does.

Riddle Answer

Answer: Pitch. In roofing, pitch (slope) determines drainage and structural integrity — too flat and you get ponding water; steep is better. In private equity, a "pitch" is the investor presentation, and a steeper growth story signals a more compelling multiple. Same word. Entirely different anxieties — and somehow, the same consequences when you get it wrong.
